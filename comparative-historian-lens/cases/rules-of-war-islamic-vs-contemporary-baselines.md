# Comparative Case: Islamic Rules of War vs Contemporary Baselines

## One-line thesis
Early Islam articulated an unusually explicit and religiously binding code of restraint in war — noncombatant immunity, bans on mutilation and gratuitous killing of captives, and limits on scorched-earth tactics — that was clearly more developed *as enforceable doctrine* than most contemporaneous 7th-century baselines; but Muslim armies repeatedly violated that code in practice (executions, enslavement, sieges), so the honest claim is "advanced ideal, unevenly enforced reality," not "uniquely humane conquest."

## Time period(s) compared
- Islamic ideal/foundational period: c. 610–660 CE (Qur'anic revelation, Prophetic instructions, Rashidun caliphate / Abu Bakr's army instructions c. 632–634 CE), with classical fiqh codification c. 8th–14th centuries.
- Comparative baselines: Roman Republic/Empire (3rd c. BCE–5th c. CE); Byzantine–Sasanian wars (esp. 602–628 CE, immediately pre-Islamic); the Crusades (1096–1291 CE); Mongol conquests (1206–1258 CE); medieval European warfare norms (broadly 9th–14th c.).

## Regions / civilizations compared
Arabia and the early caliphate; Roman/Mediterranean world; Byzantine and Sasanian Near East; Latin Christendom (Crusader states / Western Europe); the Mongol Eurasian steppe-empire.

## Benchmark dimensions
- **Scripture/ideal** (Qur'an): applies — explicit non-aggression and proportionality language.
- **Norm/Prophetic instruction**: applies — hadith prohibiting killing women/children, mutilation, treachery.
- **Law (fiqh)**: applies — classical jurists codified noncombatant categories.
- **Institution/command directive**: applies — Abu Bakr's standing orders to commanders.
- **Enforcement**: applies but WEAK/uneven — this is the key gap.
- **Lived practice**: applies — includes documented Islamic-side violations.
- **Exceptions**: applies — sieges, night raids, "military necessity," treachery cases.

## Islamic-side evidence
- **Qur'an 2:190** establishes proportionality and a non-aggression limit: "Fight in the cause of Allah ˹only˺ against those who wage war against you, but do not exceed the limits. Allah does not like transgressors." This anchors *jus in bello* restraint in scripture itself ([quran.com 2:190](https://quran.com/en/al-baqarah/190-193)). (Tier: A — Established)
- **Qur'an 2:191** is more martial and is the necessary counterweight: "Kill them wherever you come upon them and drive them out of the places from which they have driven you out. For persecution is far worse than killing." The continuation in **2:192–193** conditions cessation on the enemy ceasing ("but if they cease, then surely Allah is All-Forgiving... if they cease, then there is no aggression except against the oppressors"). Together the verses authorize hard fighting — they are NOT pacifist ([quran.com 2:191](https://quran.com/en/2/191)). (Tier: A — Established; the martial framing is part of the honest record. Verse attribution corrected: the "if they cease" clause is 2:192–193, not 2:191.)
- **Abu Bakr's instructions to the Syrian expedition (c. 633–634 CE)**, recorded in **Malik's Muwatta, Book 21 (Jihad), Hadith 10** (Arabic ref. 971): do not kill a woman, a child, or an aged, infirm person; do not cut down fruit-bearing trees; do not destroy an inhabited place; do not slaughter sheep/camels except for food; leave alone those devoted to monastic life ([sunnah.com Muwatta, Book 21](https://sunnah.com/malik/21)). This is a *command-level standing order*, not just an abstract ideal. (Tier: A for the text's transmission; Confidence: Likely for it being a literal verbatim order — see authenticity caveat below)
- **Prophetic prohibition on killing women and children**, narrated by Ibn 'Umar: a woman was found killed after a battle, "so the Messenger of Allah forbade the killing of women and children" — **Sahih al-Bukhari 3015** and **Sahih Muslim 1744a** (Book 32, Hadith 28) ([Bukhari 3015](https://sunnah.com/bukhari:3015); [Muslim 1744a](https://sunnah.com/muslim:1744a)). (Tier: A — Established)
- **The Buraydah instruction**, **Sahih Muslim 1731a**: when appointing a commander the Prophet ordered, "do not embezzle the spoils; do not break your pledge (act treacherously); and do not mutilate (the dead) bodies; do not kill the children" ([Muslim 1731a](https://sunnah.com/muslim:1731a)). Establishes bans on embezzlement, treachery, mutilation (muthla), and killing children at the moment of deployment. (Tier: A — Established)
- **Classical fiqh codified noncombatant immunity**: Hanafi, Hanbali, and Maliki jurists list protected categories — women, children, clergy/monks, the aged, the insane, farmers, serfs, the disabled — barred from being targeted unless they fight ([Islamic military jurisprudence, Wikipedia summary of school positions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_military_jurisprudence)). (Tier: B — Established as a doctrinal pattern; Wikipedia is the locator, the underlying jurists are A)

## Comparative evidence
- **Roman warfare**: Mass violence against civilians during the sack of cities was, in the sources Gabriel Baker analyzes (Polybios, Diodorus), treated as a recognized "Roman custom." The destructions of Carthage and Corinth (146 BCE) involved extraordinary violence against inhabitants; Baker frames mass killing, mass enslavement, and the destruction of cities as deliberate strategy rather than spontaneous excess ([Bryn Mawr Classical Review, review of Gabriel Baker, "Spare No One: Mass Violence in Roman Warfare," 2022](https://bmcr.brynmawr.edu/2022/2022.01.23/)). Theoretical restraints existed in antiquity, but observance was largely a matter of expedient policy, not binding law. (Tier: B — Established)
- **Crusader sack of Jerusalem, 15 July 1099**: After the city fell, large numbers of Muslim and Jewish inhabitants were massacred. The eyewitness crusader chronicler Raymond of Aguilers wrote that in "the porch of Solomon men rode in blood up to their knees and bridle reins"; the *Gesta Francorum* described men wading "in blood up to their ankles." Some defenders in the Tower / Oratory of David were spared after surrendering (per Ibn al-Athir). Fulcher of Chartres put the dead in the Temple area at roughly 10,000, while noting the true number is unknowable ([Massacre of Jerusalem (1099), Wikipedia, citing Raymond of Aguilers, Gesta Francorum, Fulcher, Ibn al-Athir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massacre_of_Jerusalem_(1099))). (Tier: B for the encyclopedia; the cited eyewitnesses are A — Established that a large massacre occurred; exact scale Contested)
- **Mongol conquests (1219–1258)**: Sieges of Nishapur, Merv, and Baghdad produced mass killing of civilians as deliberate policy/terror — "terror and mass extermination of anyone opposing them was a well-tested Mongol tactic." Medieval chroniclers (Juvayni, Ibn al-Athir, Mustawfi) give figures of hundreds of thousands to millions (e.g., Ibn al-Athir's 700,000 at Merv; Mustawfi's 800,000 at Baghdad); modern historians regard these specific numbers as vastly inflated but still describe catastrophic civilian death ([Destruction under the Mongol Empire, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Destruction_under_the_Mongol_Empire); [Siege of Baghdad, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_Baghdad)). The Mongol code centered on submission and terror, not noncombatant immunity. (Tier: B; chronicler numbers Contested, the pattern Established)
- **Byzantine–Sasanian wars (602–628 CE)**: The immediately pre-Islamic Near East saw brutal sieges and sack — e.g., the Sasanian capture of Jerusalem (614 CE) with large-scale killing and deportation, and reciprocal devastation — establishing that the baseline *contemporary* with early Islam was harsh siege warfare, not codified civilian protection. (Tier: C for this session's retrieval depth — the specific Cambridge "Law of War" chapter was not readable this session; treat as Likely, not Established. The general point is well-supported in late-antique scholarship.)

## What was advanced for its time
- **Restraint encoded as binding religious-legal duty, not optional policy.** The decisive contrast with Rome and Polybios is not that restraint was *imagined* — antiquity had theoretical limits — but that in early Islam it was framed as a command from God and the Prophet, issued as standing orders to commanders (Abu Bakr's instructions), and then codified by jurists into enforceable categories of protected persons. (Tier: Established for the doctrinal structure. NOTE: this is a doctrine-to-doctrine comparison; the practice comparison below is mixed.)
- **Explicit, enumerated noncombatant categories** (women, children, elderly, monks/clergy, later farmers/serfs/disabled) at a time when the contemporaneous Byzantine and Sasanian baseline was harsh siege warfare. (Tier: Established for doctrine)
- **Named bans on mutilation (muthla) and treachery** at the point of command appointment (Muslim 1731a) — a specific procedural restraint. (Tier: Established)
- **Environmental/economic limits** (no needless destruction of fruit trees, crops, livestock except for food). (Tier: Established as doctrine)

## What was ordinary for its time
- **Slavery and enslavement of captives** were universal across all compared civilizations, including Muslim armies — taking women and children as captives (as at Banu Qurayza) was the regional norm, not an Islamic distinctive. (Tier: Established)
- **Hard siege warfare and the killing of combatant defenders** were standard everywhere. Islamic doctrine restrained methods at the margins but did not abolish siege brutality.
- **Theoretical limits existing alongside frequent breach** — Rome, Greece, and later Europe all had *some* notional restraints (sanctuary, heralds, chivalric codes). The Islamic version was more systematized and religiously grounded, but "having a code" was not itself unique.
- **Jizya/tribute and conditional submission** as the alternative to fighting parallels Roman and Mongol submission-or-destruction logic, though Islamic terms were typically less annihilatory.

## What was morally deficient, contradictory, or unevenly enforced
**Islamic-side failures and gaps (mandatory honesty):**
- **Banu Qurayza (c. 627 CE):** After the tribe's surrender, the adult men were executed (Ibn Hisham/al-Tabari give 600–900, sometimes stated as "between 400 and 900"; revisionist scholars such as Barakat Ahmad have argued for far lower numbers, and the figures are widely doubted), and women and children were enslaved. Even granting the treachery/treaty-breach context and that the arbiter was their own chosen judge (Sa'd ibn Mu'adh, requested by the Banu Aus), this sits in deep tension with the protective ideal and is among the most contested events in the sira ([Siege of Banu Qurayza, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_of_Banu_Qurayza); [Kister, "The Massacre of the Banu Qurayza: A re-examination of a tradition," JSAI 8, 1986](https://kister.huji.ac.il/sites/default/files/pdf/banu_qurayza.pdf)). (Tier: event Established, scale Contested)
- **Enslavement of captives** by Muslim armies — including women and children — was systemic and continued for centuries. The protective rules constrained *killing* far more than they constrained *enslaving*.
- **Later Muslim conquests violated the ideals repeatedly:** sieges, sacks, and massacres occurred under various dynasties. The ideal-vs-practice gap is real and large.
- **Authenticity / legal-flexibility problem:** the foundational restraint corpus is not univocal. Jurists such as Ibn Hudayl of Granada (14th c.) and others advanced counter-positions permitting burning enemy lands, crops, and stores "provided that the imam deems these measures appropriate" — i.e., **military-necessity exceptions could swallow the restraint** ([Islamic military jurisprudence, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_military_jurisprudence)). (Tier: Established that the dispute and the counter-traditions exist.) *Note: a frequently repeated claim that al-Shafi'i specifically rejected the "ten rules of Abu Bakr" as inauthentic could not be verified against the cited source or independent search this session — exact reference to be confirmed; do not publish the al-Shafi'i attribution as fact.*
- **Enforcement was weak.** These were norms whose breach depended on the piety/discipline of commanders; there was no supranational mechanism, and violations often went unpunished.

**Comparative-side deficiencies:** Roman, Crusader, and Mongol warfare frequently lacked *any* binding civilian-protection principle, with mass killing of inhabitants treated as acceptable or even policy (Carthage, Jerusalem 1099, Nishapur/Baghdad). The point of the comparison is the doctrinal baseline, not a claim that Muslims never massacred.

**Likely / well-supported for the core thesis.** The scriptural and Prophetic restraint texts are Tier-A Established (all four hadith/Qur'an references verified against sunnah.com / quran.com this session). The comparative brutality of Rome, Crusaders, and Mongols is Established (with numbers Contested). The honest qualifier — that Islamic *practice* often fell short and that the foundational instructions face authenticity debate and necessity-exceptions — is also well-supported. The weakest links are: precise quantification (massacre scales), the verbatim authenticity of Abu Bakr's "ten rules," and the unverified al-Shafi'i attribution (now flagged).

## What this proves
- Early Islam developed an explicit, religiously binding *jus in bello* with enumerated noncombatant protections and named prohibitions (mutilation, treachery, gratuitous destruction) that, **as articulated doctrine**, was more systematic and more authoritative than the prevailing 7th-century Near Eastern and Mediterranean military baselines.
- A meaningful contrast exists between this codified ideal and the documented atrocities of contemporaneous and later non-Muslim conquests (Roman sacks, 1099 Jerusalem, Mongol sieges).

## What this does NOT prove
- It does **not** prove Muslim armies were uniquely humane or that they consistently honored these rules — they enslaved, besieged, and at times massacred (Banu Qurayza).
- It does **not** prove Islam "invented" rules of war or noncombatant immunity; antiquity (Polybios, Greek/Roman religious sanctuary norms) had earlier theoretical limits. A true *early and unusually systematized* code is the defensible claim.
- It does **not** prove the doctrine was uniformly held — jurists disagreed, and military-necessity exceptions (scorched earth "where the imam deems appropriate") could override restraint.
- It does **not** settle the historicity or scale of contested events (Banu Qurayza numbers, exact massacre tolls at Jerusalem or Baghdad).
- It does **not** license a best-doctrine-vs-worst-practice comparison: the honest frame is doctrine-to-doctrine and practice-to-practice.

## Strongest counterargument
The restraint texts may be partly retrojective and idealized, and the existence of counter-traditions permitting scorched-earth shows the corpus is not univocal. A critic can argue that (a) the binding "ideal" was assembled and emphasized by later jurists more than it governed the actual conquests, (b) enslavement of noncombatants — fully permitted — undercuts the "protection" framing, and (c) comparing best-case Islamic *doctrine* to worst-case enemy *practice* is exactly the asymmetry this project forbids. The disciplined reply is to compare doctrine-to-doctrine and practice-to-practice: even then, the Islamic doctrinal corpus is unusually explicit, but the practice record is mixed, not exceptional.
