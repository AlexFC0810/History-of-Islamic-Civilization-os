# Comparative Case: Ruler Accountability — Power as a Test, Not Immunity

## One-line thesis
Early Islam articulated a strong *ideal* that the ruler is under the law and owes obedience only insofar as he obeys God — an ideal with real institutional teeth (an independent scholarly class, mazalim courts) but one that was repeatedly overridden in *lived practice* by dynastic absolutism, hereditary succession, and even a state inquisition; the honest verdict is "a robust accountability norm, unevenly and often poorly enforced," not "constitutional government 1,000 years early."

## Time period(s) compared
- Islamic ideal/formative: Prophetic era and Rashidun caliphate (632–661 CE).
- Islamic institutional development and failures: Umayyad (661–750) and Abbasid (750–1258) caliphates, including the Mihna (833–c.851).
- Comparative baselines: Late Roman/Byzantine imperial ideology (4th–11th c. CE) and early-modern European "divine right" absolutism (16th–17th c. CE), used as a deliberately later contrast point and flagged as such.

## Regions / civilizations compared
- The Islamic heartlands (Arabia, Iraq, Syria) under the Rashidun, Umayyads, and Abbasids.
- The Byzantine (Eastern Roman) Empire.
- Early-modern Western Europe (for the "absolutism" contrast — chronologically *later*, so used only to locate ideas, not to claim Islamic priority).

## Benchmark dimensions
- **Scripture / norm:** APPLIES — Qur'anic justice commands; the "obedience only in the good" hadith.
- **Foundational political precedent:** APPLIES — Abu Bakr's inaugural address (as a transmitted literary report).
- **Institution:** APPLIES — the independent scholarly class (ulama) as interpreters of law; mazalim (grievance) courts.
- **Enforcement:** PARTIAL/WEAK — depended on a ruler's willingness; no standing mechanism could depose a sitting caliph.
- **Lived practice:** APPLIES (and is where the ideal most often failed) — hereditary succession, suppression of dissent, the Mihna.
- **Exceptions:** APPLIES — the Mihna is the sharpest counter-instance, where the state coerced scholars rather than being checked by them.

## Islamic-side evidence
- **Scriptural duty of justice over self-interest.** Qur'an 4:135 commands believers to "stand firm for justice as witnesses for Allah even if it is against yourselves, your parents, or close relatives" ([quran.com 4:135](https://quran.com/en/4/135)). This is the moral substrate for the claim that no one — including the powerful — is above accountability. (Tier: A; Established)
- **Obedience to authority is conditional, not absolute.** Qur'an 4:59 commands obedience to "those in authority among you," but immediately subordinates that to referral back to "Allah and His Messenger" in disputes ([quran.com 4:59](https://quran.com/en/4/59)) — i.e., authority is bounded, not autonomous. (Tier: A; Established, though the *scope* of the limit is interpreted variously.)
- **"No obedience in disobedience to God."** Sahih al-Bukhari 7257 (USC-MSA: Vol. 9, Book 91, Hadith 363): "No obedience for evil deeds; obedience is required only in what is good" ([sunnah.com/bukhari:7257](https://sunnah.com/bukhari:7257)). The same principle is stated even more explicitly in Sahih al-Bukhari 7144 (USC-MSA Vol. 9, Book 89, Hadith 258): a Muslim must obey "as long as his orders involve not one in disobedience [to Allah], but if an act of disobedience is imposed one should not listen to it or obey it" ([sunnah.com/bukhari:7144](https://sunnah.com/bukhari:7144)). A related report is also transmitted via Sahih Muslim (commonly cited around no. 1840 — *exact reference and wording to be confirmed against the Arabic; not relied upon here*). (Tier: A; Established as authenticated hadith. Caveat: the principle authorizes *disobedience to an unlawful command*, not removal of the ruler.)
- **Abu Bakr's inaugural address (632 CE).** On accepting the caliphate Abu Bakr is reported to have said: "Obey me so long as I obey God and His Messenger; but if I disobey God and His Messenger, you owe me no obedience," and "If I do well, help me; and if I do wrong, set me right." The most commonly cited transmission is Ibn Hisham's *al-Sira al-Nabawiyya* (the account of Saqifat Bani Sa'ida), and the report recurs across the standard sira/tarikh literature ([abuaminaelias.com summary, sourcing Ibn Hisham](https://www.abuaminaelias.com/dailyhadithonline/2012/09/13/opening-speech-of-abu-bakr-caliph/)). (Tier: A for the report's presence in the classical corpus / C for this web summary; Likely-but-transmission-caveated — this is a *literary historical report*, not a documentary record, preserved and amplified by a tradition with an interest in idealizing the Rashidun.)
- **The ulama as a structural check.** Noah Feldman argues the classical Sunni order was one in which "the classical Islamic constitution governed through and was legitimated by law," with "executive power... balanced by the scholars who interpreted and administered the shari'a" — judicial authority flowed from the caliph but the *content of the law* came from an independent learned class, giving them leverage over rulers ([Princeton UP](https://press.princeton.edu/books/paperback/9780691156248/the-fall-and-rise-of-the-islamic-state); [Harvard Law summary](https://hls.harvard.edu/today/in-new-book-feldman-looks-behind-the-calls-for-sharia/)). (Tier: A; Likely/Contested — Feldman's "rule of law" framing is influential but debated; critics note the scholars had no power to *remove* a ruler.)
- **Mazalim (court of grievances).** Mazalim jurisdiction allowed subjects to bring complaints "against government officials, governors and rulers, princes, army commanders and the judges themselves," with a stated objective "to subjugate the government itself to the rule of law" (Hashim Kamali, summarized at [islamciv.com](https://islamciv.com/2024/04/26/the-diwan-al-ma%E1%BA%93alim-court-of-grievances-by-hashim-kamali/); cf. Jonathan A.C. Brown, *Justice and Islamic Law: Mazalim Courts and Legal Reform*, [Oneworld](https://oneworld-publications.com/work/justice-and-islamic-law/), the first major English-language study of these courts). The institution was formalized in the early Abbasid period (by roughly the mid-9th century), with earlier precedents (e.g., under Umar b. Abd al-Aziz, early 8th c.). (Tier: A/B; Established that the institution existed; Contested how independent it was, since the ruler or his appointee often presided.)
- **Period framing.** Crone's *God's Rule: Six Centuries of Medieval Islamic Political Thought* situates these debates within medieval Islamic thinking about whether and why government must be based on religious law ([Columbia UP](https://cup.columbia.edu/book/gods-rule-government-and-islam/9780231132909/)). (Tier: A; Established as scholarship.)

## Comparative evidence
- **Umayyad shift toward absolutism (within Islam itself).** Mu'awiya introduced *hereditary* succession when he nominated his son Yazid as successor in 676 CE — described in the standard literature as "turning the office of the caliph into a kingship" ([Wikipedia: Umayyad Caliphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate)). Separately, Crone and Hinds argue that Umayyad caliphs advanced the self-aggrandizing title *khalifat Allah* ("deputy of God") rather than the Rashidun-associated *khalifat rasul Allah* ("successor of the Messenger of God"), claiming religious as well as political authority (Patricia Crone & Martin Hinds, *God's Caliph: Religious Authority in the First Centuries of Islam*, CUP 1986, ch. "The title khalifat Allah"; [overview](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/God's_Caliph)). Later tradition recognized only Umar II as a true *khalifa* rather than a worldly *malik* (king) — an internal critique distinguishing *khilafa* from *mulk*. This is the *internal* baseline: the Islamic tradition's own critique of its own rulers. (Tier: B for hereditary succession; A for the *khalifat Allah* scholarship; the "tyranny" valuation of *mulk* is itself a later normative judgment.)
- **Byzantine imperial ideology (mixed picture).** The Byzantine emperor was presented as "an absolute monarch" "appointed by God," surrounded by sacralizing court ceremony (proskynesis) ([World History Encyclopedia: Byzantine Emperor](https://www.worldhistory.org/Byzantine_Emperor/), Tier B — this is the sacral/absolutist strand). A *separate, more contested* strand of Roman/Byzantine political theory held the emperor a mortal answerable to God and to the *politeia*, with imperial power deriving in law from the people (a reading built on a Justinianic legal text and the *anaxios*/"unworthy" acclamation) — but the source retrieved for this is a non-peer-reviewed personal academic site ([friesian.com](https://friesian.com/republic.htm), Tier C), drawing on Anthony Kaldellis's *The Byzantine Republic* (Harvard UP, 2015, A-grade but not independently retrieved here). So the most that can be said with discipline is that *some* Roman/Byzantine theory contained an accountability strand — i.e., the Islamic ideal is not the *only* late-antique tradition with one — but this comparative claim is **Contested**, not settled. (Tier: B for the sacral strand; C for the accountability strand as currently sourced.)
- **Early-modern European "divine right" (a LATER contrast).** The doctrine that the king "is not accountable to any earthly authority... because their right to rule is derived from divine authority" is a *post-Reformation* Western development "culminating in the Age of Absolutism (c. 1610 – c. 1789)," post-dating the Islamic formative period by roughly a millennium ([Wikipedia: Divine right of kings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divine_right_of_kings)). It is therefore wrong to say Islam "beat Europe to accountability" — the honest comparison is best-to-best (Rashidun ideal vs. Roman/Byzantine accountability strands), not Rashidun-ideal vs. Louis XIV. (Tier: B; Established, with explicit chronology caveat.)

## What was advanced for its time
- A **publicly articulated conditionality of obedience** — the ruler is owed obedience *only* within God's law, voiced as a foundational political precedent (Abu Bakr) and grounded in authenticated hadith. As a *stated norm at the moment of founding*, this is striking.
- The **separation of law-making (scholars) from law-enforcing (ruler)**: because the law's content was held by an independent learned class, no caliph could simply legislate his will into legitimacy (Feldman). This is a genuine structural constraint, not just rhetoric — though contested, and never amounting to a removal power.
- A **dedicated grievance jurisdiction (mazalim)** whose stated rationale was to subject the state itself to law and to hear complaints against officials and even rulers.

## What was ordinary for its time
- **Religious legitimation of rulers** was the norm everywhere (Byzantium, Sasanian Iran, Latin Christendom); Islam was not unusual in tying authority to the divine. Indeed, some Umayyad caliphs pushed *toward* sacral kingship (*khalifat Allah*), not away from it.
- **Accountability "to God"** as a moral check on monarchs was a shared late-antique idea, not an Islamic monopoly — a strand of Roman/Byzantine theory likewise held the emperor answerable to God and the *politeia* (a contested but real reading).
- **The gap between an accountability ideal and an actual enforcement mechanism** was also ordinary: like most premodern polities, the Islamic order lacked any routine, non-violent procedure to *remove* a bad ruler.

## What was morally deficient, contradictory, or unevenly enforced
*(Islamic-side failures stated plainly — this is the core of the honest reckoning.)*
- **Hereditary dynastic kingship displaced the consultative ideal fast.** Within ~44 years of the Prophet's death, Mu'awiya converted succession into hereditary rule (Yazid, 676), and Umayyad ideology advanced the self-exalting *khalifat Allah*. The accountability ideal was *already* being hollowed out by the first dynasty.
- **The Mihna (inquisition, 833–c.851).** Far from scholars checking the caliph, the Abbasid caliph al-Ma'mun and his successors (al-Mu'tasim, al-Wathiq) *coerced* scholars and judges to affirm a state theological doctrine (the Qur'an's "createdness"), imprisoning and flogging dissenters such as Ahmad ibn Hanbal ([Wikipedia: Mihna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mihna)). This is the textbook case where accountability ran *backwards* — the state disciplining the scholars. Any honest presentation of "ulama as a check" must put the Mihna on the same page.
- **No deposition mechanism.** The dominant Sunni juristic tendency, fearing *fitna* (civil strife), generally counseled patience under unjust rulers rather than rebellion. The conditional-obedience principle licensed *refusing an unlawful order*, but rarely *removing* the ruler — so accountability was real at the margins and weak at the center.
- **Mazalim's independence is questionable.** Because the ruler or his appointee frequently presided over the grievance court, it could function as much as a tool of royal image-management as a genuine constraint. Calling it "the caliph could be judged" overstates how it usually worked.
- **Idealization bias in the sources.** The most-quoted accountability material (Abu Bakr's speech, Rashidun virtue) survives through a later tradition with strong incentives to portray the "Rightly Guided" era as a golden age. Treat these as normative literature, not neutral transcripts.

## Confidence rating (overall)
**Moderate-to-high for the existence of a strong accountability *ideal and norm* (A-grade scripture and hadith; A-grade scholarship from Feldman, Crone, Brown). Lower for claims about *effective enforcement*: the institutions existed but were inconsistently independent, and the Mihna shows the system could invert. Lower still for the *comparative* Byzantine-accountability point, which currently rests on a Tier C source.** Net: confident in "Islam articulated and institutionalized a serious accountability ideal"; *not* confident in any claim that it reliably constrained sitting rulers.

## What this proves
- That from its founding, the Islamic political tradition contained an explicit, scripturally anchored principle that *power is bounded by God's law and obedience is conditional*, and that it later built institutions (an independent scholarly class, mazalim courts) expressing that principle.
- That the common Western mental image of premodern Islamic rule as pure, unchecked "Oriental despotism" is historically simplistic.

## What this does NOT prove
- It does **not** prove the caliphate was a "rule-of-law state" in the modern constitutional sense, nor that rulers were routinely held accountable in practice.
- It does **not** prove Islamic priority over other civilizations; accountability-to-God ideas existed (in contested form) in Byzantium, and the "absolute, answerable-only-to-God" doctrine Islam is often contrasted with is *early-modern European*, not its contemporary.
- It does **not** prove the existence of any mechanism to depose an unjust ruler — generally there was none, and the dominant counsel was patience.
- It does **not** prove the Islamic tradition trended *toward* accountability over time — within the first dynasty it trended toward sacral kingship (*khalifat Allah*) and later produced a state inquisition (the Mihna).
- The Abu Bakr quotation does **not** function as documentary evidence; it is a transmitted historical report (via Ibn Hisham and the later corpus) carrying idealization.

## Strongest counterargument
"The accountability 'ideal' is mostly retrospective hagiography. The hard institutional record is the opposite: hereditary dynasties within a generation, caliphs who advanced a 'deputy of God' title, a caliph-run inquisition that tortured the very scholars supposedly checking him, quietist jurists who told subjects to endure tyranny to avoid *fitna*, and grievance courts presided over by the ruler's own appointees. A norm that produces the Mihna is not a functioning check; it is a moral aspiration that the political order routinely defeated." A fair case must concede most of this and rest its claim on the *norm and the partial institutions*, not on enforcement outcomes.

## Content angles
1. **"Obey me only as long as I obey God."** Open on Abu Bakr's first speech as caliph (a transmitted report, via Ibn Hisham) — then immediately ask: did it survive contact with power? (Honest arc: norm → erosion.)
2. **The inquisition Muslims don't advertise.** Use the Mihna to model intellectual honesty: the same tradition that idealizes scholar-vs-tyrant also produced caliphs who tortured scholars.
3. **"Deputy of God" vs. "successor of the Prophet."** One title shift (*khalifat Allah*, per Crone & Hinds) as a window into how a republic-of-virtue ideal slid toward sacral kingship.
4. **Who owned the law?** Feldman's insight — the ruler enforced law but didn't *make* it — as the most defensible "early separation of powers" claim, with the caveats (no removal power) attached.
5. **Best-to-best, not best-to-worst.** Compare the Rashidun *ideal* to the Byzantine *ideal* (both contained an accountability strand) instead of to Louis XIV — and show why the honest comparison is more interesting than the triumphalist one.
6. **A test, not immunity.** The thesis line: scripture frames power as a trust one will be *questioned* about — then the historical record shows how rarely that questioning had teeth.
