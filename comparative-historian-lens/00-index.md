# Comparative Civilization Benchmark Atlas

## Parent meta

This is the applied research implementation of **Historical Calibration / the Comparative Historian Lens** from the Intelligence Kernel / Meta Atlas.

## Core thesis

People often cannot understand the moral or civilizational significance of a practice unless they have a benchmark. Historical facts need calibrated comparison.

## Core question

> Compared to whom, when, under what conditions, and by what moral/institutional benchmark?

## Purpose

Build a research-backed comparative history atlas that evaluates Islamic civilization in context against contemporary civilizations and later civilizational developments.

This is not a whitewashing project. It is a source-first calibration project.

## Initial comparison domains

1. Animal welfare and treatment of non-human creatures.
2. Women's rights: property, inheritance, marriage, divorce, education, legal standing.
3. Treatment of the vulnerable: orphans, widows, poor, travelers, sick, elderly, disabled.
4. Slavery, captives, manumission, racial/ethnic status, protections, and abuses.
5. Hospitals, public health, medical ethics, and care institutions.
6. Religious minorities and pluralism.
7. Knowledge systems: translation, libraries, science, preservation, debate culture.
8. War ethics: prisoners, civilians, treaties, restraint, crops, animals, trees.
9. Market ethics: hisbah, fraud, weights/measures, animal burdening, labor protections.
10. Moral failures and contradictions inside Islamic societies themselves.

## Methodological guardrails

- Do not whitewash Islamic civilization.
- Do not cherry-pick the best Islamic examples against the worst examples elsewhere.
- Compare similar time periods and institutions where possible.
- Separate scripture/norm from law, institution, enforcement, and lived practice.
- Separate prophetic/early Islamic ideals from Abbasid/Ottoman/Andalusian/Mughal/etc. implementations.
- Use confidence tiers: established, likely, contested, anecdotal, weak.
- Include failures and regressions.
- Make chronology explicit.
- Always include what this does NOT prove.

## Master line

> The goal is not to prove a civilization was perfect. The goal is to reveal what civilizations saw, protected, neglected, normalized, or failed to see.
