# Prophet Muhammad ﷺ — The Final Sermon

Khutbat al-Wadāʿ, "The Farewell Sermon," delivered during the Farewell Pilgrimage (Hajjat al-Wadāʿ) in the Uranah valley at the foot of Mount ʿArafāt on 9 Dhul-Hijjah, 10 AH / March 632 CE, before an audience commonly estimated at 100,000+ pilgrims.

## One-line claim

Months before his death, in front of more than a hundred thousand people, the Prophet ﷺ delivered a compressed moral constitution that abolished racial superiority, ended blood-vengeance and usury, affirmed the sanctity of life and property, and bound every listener to deliver the message onward.

## What happened

Standing at ʿArafāt during the only pilgrimage he led after the conquest of Mecca, the Prophet ﷺ addressed the largest gathering of his life. The sermon was paraphrased into clusters of declarations across the canonical Hadith collections (notably Bukhari, Muslim, Tirmidhī, Ahmad's *Musnad*, and Ibn Ishaq's *Sīra*). The clusters include:

- The sanctity of life, property, and honor — "as sacred as this day, this month, and this city."
- Abolition of pre-Islamic blood vengeance, starting with claims belonging to his own family.
- Abolition of *ribā* (usury), starting with claims belonging to his own uncle al-ʿAbbās.
- A direct strike at tribal and racial hierarchy: "No Arab has superiority over a non-Arab, nor a non-Arab over an Arab. No white has superiority over a black, nor a black over a white — except by *taqwā* (God-consciousness) and good action."
- Instructions to treat women with kindness, recognize mutual rights between spouses, and uphold the rights of women generally.
- A reminder that all believers are brothers, that taking what is not freely given is forbidden.
- The charge: "Let those who are present convey to those who are absent."
- A closing call-and-response: "Have I conveyed the message? O Allah, bear witness."

## Why it's Pareto

It satisfies five of the six criteria.

- **Recalibrates without arguing.** A 7th-century Arabian speech that explicitly abolishes racial and ethnic superiority disarms the modern frame that anti-racism is a post-Enlightenment Western invention.
- **Survives scale.** Compressed into roughly the length of a magazine column, transmitted through multiple parallel chains for fourteen centuries.
- **Crosses tribal lines.** Muslim audiences encounter it as scripture-adjacent canon. Christian, secular, and historically curious audiences encounter it as an unexpectedly modern moral document from an unexpectedly early date.
- **Has documentary footing.** Multiple canonical chains. The sermon is one of the most rigorously transmitted public statements in pre-modern history.
- **Lifts a specific propaganda layer.** It contradicts the inherited story that universal human dignity is a Western and modern invention.

## Primary sources

- Sahih al-Bukhari — multiple narrations referencing the Farewell Hajj.
- Sahih Muslim — Kitāb al-Hajj.
- Musnad Ahmad ibn Hanbal — extended narrations including the anti-racism cluster.
- Sunan al-Tirmidhī, Sunan Abī Dāwūd, Sunan an-Nasāʾī.
- Ibn Hishām's redaction of Ibn Isḥāq's *Sīra*.
- Al-Ṭabarī's *Tārīkh al-Rusul wa al-Mulūk*.

Translation note: there is no single "verbatim text." The sermon survives as a constellation of paraphrased clusters across narrators. Public-facing renderings should make this clear and avoid presenting one composite as the original.
