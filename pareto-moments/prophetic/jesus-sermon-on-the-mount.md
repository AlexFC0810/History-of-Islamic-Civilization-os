# Jesus — The Sermon on the Mount

The sermon delivered on a hillside in Galilee, recorded in the Gospel of Matthew chapters 5–7 (with parallels in Luke 6, "Sermon on the Plain"), c. 30 CE.

## One-line claim

The most compressed and most quoted moral teaching in the Western tradition turns the entire transactional logic of empire inside-out — and it is delivered by a prophet whom Islamic scripture also reveres.

## What happened

Jesus ascends a hillside, sits, and delivers the longest continuous teaching attributed to him in the New Testament. The sermon is structured in clusters:

- **The Beatitudes** — blessings on the poor in spirit, those who mourn, the meek, those who hunger and thirst for righteousness, the merciful, the pure in heart, the peacemakers, and the persecuted.
- **Salt and light** — the disciple's role as preserver and witness.
- **Fulfillment, not abolition** — Jesus locates himself within the prophetic tradition, not against it.
- **Interior ethics** — anger as the seed of murder, lust as the seed of adultery, the moral life pushed inward beyond outward compliance.
- **Honest speech** — let your "yes" be yes and your "no" be no; oaths are unnecessary for honest people.
- **Non-retaliation** — "turn the other cheek," "go the second mile," "give your cloak as well." The arithmetic of vengeance is broken.
- **Love your enemies** — and pray for those who persecute you. The moral horizon is universalized beyond tribe.
- **Almsgiving, prayer, and fasting in secret** — religion not performed for human witness.
- **Treasures in heaven** — the orientation of the heart determines the orientation of the life.
- **Do not be anxious** — providence over scarcity.
- **The narrow gate, the good tree, and the wise builder** — the closing trio: a teaching is judged by its fruit, and the house that endures is the one built on practice, not applause.

## Why it's Pareto

It satisfies all six criteria.

- **Recalibrates without arguing.** Read aloud cold, it disarms partisan reflexes. The political map collapses; the moral map appears.
- **Survives scale.** Two millennia of transmission, translation, and citation.
- **Crosses tribal lines.** Christians inherit it as central scripture. Muslims revere ʿĪsā (Jesus) as a prophet; the sermon's content is consonant with Qurʾānic ethics. Secular moral philosophers from Kant to Tolstoy to Gandhi to MLK treated it as foundational.
- **Has documentary footing.** Canonical Gospels, multiple early manuscripts.
- **Reveals fruit.** The Beatitudes, "love your enemies," and "judge not" are among the most quoted sentences in human history.
- **Lifts a specific propaganda layer.** It contradicts the modern reduction of Christianity to either (a) Western civilizational marker, (b) culture-war signifier, or (c) imperial-era theology. The sermon is older than all three uses and undermines all three.

## Primary sources

- Gospel of Matthew, chapters 5–7 (the long form).
- Gospel of Luke, chapter 6 (the parallel "Sermon on the Plain").
- Qurʾān 5:46 — affirming the Gospel given to ʿĪsā.
- Qurʾān 3:42–55, 19 (Sūrat Maryam), 4:171, 5:75–78 — the Islamic frame on ʿĪsā and Maryam.

## Evidence grade

- The sermon as recorded in Matthew — **A** as canonical Christian scripture.
- Historical reconstruction of the exact words spoken on the hillside — **C**, as with all reconstructions of pre-recording oral teaching. This does not weaken the moral content; it constrains claims of verbatim quotation.
- Bridge claim (Islam reveres Jesus, Mary, the Gospel) — **A**, see `research-ledger/CLAIMS_TO_VERIFY.md` entry "Jesus and Mary in Islam."

## Public wording constraints

- Distinguish the sermon as Christian scripture, the sermon as moral teaching, and the historical question of exact wording. Conflating these forfeits trust.
- Do not weaponize the sermon against Christians (e.g., "you don't actually do this"). The sermon is bridge fuel, not a polemic tool.
- Do not Islamicize Jesus in framing. The Qurʾānic frame is real and load-bearing; it is offered as a bridge, not a takeover.
- Honor original honorifics: Christians say "Jesus" or "Christ," Muslims say "ʿĪsā" or "ʿĪsā ibn Maryam, peace be upon him." Use the audience's frame, gloss the other once.
