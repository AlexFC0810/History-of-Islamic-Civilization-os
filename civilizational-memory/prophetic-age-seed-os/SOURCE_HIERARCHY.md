# Source Hierarchy

## Purpose

The Prophetic age requires special source discipline. Public claims should not treat every report as equally certain.

## Source Ladder

### 1. Qur'an

Highest central source for worldview, moral commands, theological architecture, and broad community formation.

### 2. Sound hadith

Strongest source family for sayings, actions, practices, and Prophetic example, with grading and context.

### 3. Sirah and maghazi

Essential narrative layer for biography, sequence, and events. Must be handled with careful source awareness.

### 4. Tafsir and fiqh

Interpretive tradition for how revelation and reports were understood, debated, and applied.

### 5. Modern academic history

Useful for chronology, source criticism, context, comparative history, archaeology, epigraphy, and late antique setting.

### 6. Public wording

Public content should use safer wording than internal synthesis.

## Claim Grades

- **A:** directly anchored in Qur'an or highly reliable hadith with low controversy.
- **B:** strongly supported by traditional sources and/or academic consensus, but needs context.
- **C:** promising synthesis; needs further source work.
- **D:** do not publish yet.

## Key Caution

The Qur'an gives the strongest access to the message and worldview. Later biography gives narrative detail but must be handled with awareness of transmission layers.

## Working Rule

> **The more viral the claim, the more source-hardened it must be.**
