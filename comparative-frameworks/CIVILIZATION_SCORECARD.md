# Civilization Scorecard

## Purpose
Create a fair, source-hardened comparison framework for evaluating civilizations by historical fruit, not civilizational self-image.

This is not designed to flatter Islamic civilization or attack others. It is designed to ask better questions.

## Core Evaluation Question
Which systems increased human capability while reducing cruelty, exploitation, arrogance, and social collapse over time?

## Scoring Method
Each dimension should be graded only after research hardening.

Suggested grades:
- **A:** strong evidence of scalable, institutionalized moral/intellectual excellence
- **B:** meaningful evidence, but partial or uneven implementation
- **C:** mixed record or limited scope
- **D:** weak record, high contradiction, or mainly rhetorical
- **F:** systemic cruelty, hypocrisy, or collapse under stress
- **Unknown:** needs research

## Dimensions

### 1. Animal Welfare
Questions:
- Were animals treated as morally relevant beings?
- Were there legal or social restrictions on cruelty?
- Were work animals protected from abuse or overwork?
- Was suffering minimized beyond mere property protection?

### 2. Orphans, Widows, and Vulnerable People
Questions:
- Were the vulnerable given legal protections?
- Were their rights enforceable?
- Was care institutionalized or merely sentimental?

### 3. Poor Relief and Charity Infrastructure
Questions:
- Was charity voluntary, obligatory, or structurally embedded?
- Did the system reduce dependency on ruler benevolence?
- Were there independent funding mechanisms?

### 4. Rules of War
Questions:
- Were noncombatants protected?
- Were crops, animals, water sources, and religious figures protected?
- Were captives, wounded enemies, and surrendered soldiers protected?
- Was violence treated as glory, necessity, or moral danger?

### 5. Ruler Accountability
Questions:
- Were rulers morally or legally constrained?
- Could scholars, judges, or citizens critique rulers?
- Did power increase accountability or immunity?

### 6. Religious Coexistence and Forced Conversion
Questions:
- Was forced conversion doctrinally prohibited, practiced, tolerated, or normalized?
- How were religious minorities treated legally and socially?
- Distinguish conquest, governance, and conversion.

### 7. Women’s Legal Status
Questions:
- Could women own property?
- Could women inherit?
- Were consent and marriage rights recognized?
- How did theory compare with practice?

### 8. Slavery and Human Commodification
Questions:
- Was slavery normalized, restricted, reformed, or abolition-oriented?
- Were manumission mechanisms incentivized?
- Were enslaved people granted rights?
- How did the record compare with surrounding civilizations?

### 9. Knowledge Systems
Questions:
- Was knowledge pursued as sacred, practical, elite, or dangerous?
- Were scholars funded independently?
- Were foreign ideas translated, tested, rejected, or suppressed?

### 10. Hygiene, Health, and Medicine
Questions:
- Were hospitals institutionalized?
- Was care accessible?
- Were mental health, sanitation, and prevention taken seriously?

### 11. Human Failure Assumptions
Questions:
- Did the system assume humans are corruptible?
- Did it create guardrails around power, wealth, sex, violence, and ego?

### 12. Internal Self-Correction
Questions:
- Could the system critique itself from within?
- Were excess, hypocrisy, corruption, and arrogance named as failures?
